# Pricing Strategy

## Introduction

Pricing is one of the most powerful levers in business—a 1% improvement in price typically has 3-10x more impact on profitability than a 1% improvement in volume or costs. Yet pricing is often set arbitrarily, based on costs rather than value, or simply by copying competitors.

Strategic pricing aligns price with customer value, business strategy, and market positioning. This document provides Synth with frameworks for developing pricing strategies that maximize revenue, profit, and strategic positioning.

### Why Pricing Strategy Matters

**Impact of Pricing:**
- Most direct path to profitability (no additional costs)
- Shapes customer perception of value
- Determines which customers you attract
- Influences competitive positioning
- Affects sales cycle length and close rates

**Cost of Poor Pricing:**
- Money left on the table (charging less than customers would pay)
- Attracting wrong customers (price-sensitive vs value-seeking)
- Unsustainable unit economics
- Inability to fund growth or product development
- Positioning as "cheap" or "premium" misaligned with strategy

**Benefits of Strategic Pricing:**
- Optimal revenue capture
- Attract ideal customers
- Sustainable margins
- Clear value communication
- Competitive differentiation

## Pricing Foundations

### Cost-Plus Pricing

**Definition:** Price = Cost + Margin

**Formula:**
```
Price = (Cost to Deliver) / (1 - Desired Margin %)
```

**Example:**
```
Cost to deliver: $40
Desired margin: 40%
Price = $40 / (1 - 0.40) = $40 / 0.60 = $66.67
```

**When to use:**
- Commoditized products with known costs
- Low differentiation markets
- Regulated industries
- Internal pricing (transfer pricing)

**Limitations:**
- Ignores customer willingness to pay
- Leaves money on the table if value exceeds cost
- No incentive to reduce costs
- Doesn't account for competitive dynamics

---

### Competitive Pricing

**Definition:** Price based on what competitors charge.

**Approaches:**
- **At parity:** Same price as competitors
- **Premium:** 10-30% above competitors
- **Discount:** 10-30% below competitors

**When to use:**
- Established, mature markets
- Clear competitive set
- Differentiation is difficult
- Customers compare prices directly

**Limitations:**
- Assumes competitors have optimal pricing (often they don't)
- Ignores your unique value proposition
- Can trigger price wars
- Doesn't maximize value capture

---

### Value-Based Pricing

**Definition:** Price based on the economic value delivered to customers.

**Formula:**
```
Price = (Value Created for Customer) × (Value Capture %)
```

**Example:**
```
Your solution saves customer $100,000/year in operational costs
You capture 30% of that value
Price = $100,000 × 0.30 = $30,000/year
```

**When to use:**
- Clear, quantifiable value proposition
- Differentiated offering
- Customers make buying decisions on ROI
- B2B solutions with measurable impact

**Advantages:**
- Aligns price with value (fairness perception)
- Maximizes revenue capture
- Justifies premium positioning
- Focuses on value communication, not features

**Requirements:**
- Ability to articulate and quantify value
- Customer understanding of their costs/metrics
- Sales team equipped to have value conversations

## Value-Based Pricing Framework

### Step 1: Identify Value Drivers

**What value does your solution create?**

**Cost Savings:**
- Reduced labor costs
- Lower operational expenses
- Decreased error rates
- Reduced overhead

**Revenue Generation:**
- Increased sales conversion
- Higher average order value
- More repeat purchases
- Market expansion

**Risk Reduction:**
- Compliance assurance
- Security improvements
- Downtime prevention
- Reputation protection

**Time Savings:**
- Faster processes
- Reduced manual work
- Quicker time-to-market
- Efficiency gains

**Quality Improvements:**
- Better outcomes
- Higher customer satisfaction
- Reduced rework
- Enhanced performance

---

### Step 2: Quantify Value

**Make value concrete and measurable.**

**Example: Marketing Automation Platform**

**Value Driver:** Reduces time spent on email campaigns

**Quantification:**
```
Current state:
- Marketing team: 3 people
- Hours per week on email campaigns: 20 hours
- Hourly cost (salary + overhead): $50
- Annual cost: 3 × 20 × 52 × $50 = $156,000

With solution:
- Hours per week: 5 hours (75% reduction)
- Annual cost: 3 × 5 × 52 × $50 = $39,000

Value created: $156,000 - $39,000 = $117,000/year
```

**Value Driver:** Increases email conversion rates

**Quantification:**
```
Current conversion rate: 2%
Email list: 100,000 subscribers
Revenue per conversion: $500
Current revenue: 100,000 × 0.02 × $500 = $1,000,000/year

With solution (better targeting/personalization):
Conversion rate: 2.5% (25% improvement)
New revenue: 100,000 × 0.025 × $500 = $1,250,000/year

Value created: $250,000/year additional revenue
```

**Total Value Created:** $117,000 + $250,000 = $367,000/year

**Pricing Calculation:**
```
Capture 20-30% of value created
Price range: $73,000 - $110,000/year
Set at: $89,000/year (or $7,500/month)
```

---

### Step 3: Determine Value Capture Percentage

**How much of the created value should you capture?**

**Factors:**

**Competition Level:**
- Monopoly/unique: 40-60% capture
- Differentiated: 20-40% capture
- Competitive: 10-20% capture

**Customer Risk:**
- Proven results: 30-50% capture
- Some uncertainty: 20-30% capture
- Unproven: 10-20% capture

**Implementation Effort:**
- Plug-and-play: Higher capture
- Complex integration: Lower capture (customer bears cost)

**Strategic Importance:**
- Land-and-expand strategy: Lower upfront capture
- Maximize immediate revenue: Higher capture

**Typical Range:** 20-40% of value created

---

### Step 4: Validate Willingness to Pay

**Test whether customers will actually pay your price.**

**Methods:**

**Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter:**
Ask four questions:
1. At what price would this be so expensive you wouldn't consider it?
2. At what price would this be expensive but you'd consider it?
3. At what price would this be a bargain?
4. At what price would this be so cheap you'd question quality?

Plot responses to find optimal price range.

**Conjoint Analysis:**
Present customers with feature/price combinations
Measure which they prefer
Derives willingness to pay for specific features

**A/B Price Testing:**
Show different prices to different customer segments
Measure conversion and revenue
Optimize based on actual behavior

**Customer Interviews:**
"If this solution delivers [outcome], what would that be worth to you?"
"What's your budget for solving [problem]?"
"How do you justify purchases like this internally?"

## Pricing Models

### Usage-Based Pricing

**Definition:** Charge based on usage (API calls, seats, volume, transactions).

**Examples:**
- AWS: Pay for compute hours used
- Twilio: Pay per API call
- Stripe: Percentage of transaction value

**Advantages:**
- Aligns cost with value received
- Low barrier to entry (start small)
- Revenue grows with customer growth
- Perceived fairness

**Challenges:**
- Revenue unpredictability
- Billing complexity
- May limit customer usage
- Lower revenue from light users

**When to use:**
- Value scales with usage
- Want product-led growth
- Infrastructure/platform businesses
- Customers have variable needs

**Pricing design:**
```
Tiered rates (volume discounts):
- First 10,000 API calls: $0.01 per call
- Next 40,000 calls: $0.008 per call
- 50,000+ calls: $0.006 per call
```

---

### Subscription Pricing

**Definition:** Fixed recurring fee (monthly or annual).

**Examples:**
- Netflix: $15/month unlimited streaming
- SaaS tools: $50/user/month

**Advantages:**
- Predictable recurring revenue
- Simple for customers to budget
- Lower friction than usage-based
- Encourages unlimited usage

**Challenges:**
- May not align with value delivered
- One-size-fits-all doesn't fit all customers
- Requires active retention efforts
- Limited expansion revenue

**When to use:**
- Value doesn't vary significantly by usage
- Want predictable revenue
- Encourage heavy usage for stickiness
- Simple go-to-market

---

### Tiered Pricing

**Definition:** Multiple packages at different price points with feature/limit differentiation.

**Examples:**
```
Starter: $29/month
- Up to 1,000 contacts
- Basic features
- Email support

Professional: $79/month
- Up to 10,000 contacts
- Advanced features
- Phone support

Enterprise: $299/month
- Unlimited contacts
- All features
- Dedicated support
```

**Advantages:**
- Captures different willingness to pay
- Clear upgrade path
- Anchors (makes middle tier look reasonable)
- Segments customers by needs

**Challenges:**
- Feature packaging complexity
- Risk of tier cannibalization
- Customers may feel "nickel-and-dimed"

**When to use:**
- Clear customer segments with different needs
- Want to maximize revenue per customer
- Have feature set that supports differentiation

**Design principles:**
- 3-4 tiers maximum
- Make middle tier most attractive (anchor effect)
- Name tiers by customer type, not adjectives (SMB, Enterprise vs Basic, Premium)
- Ensure meaningful differentiation between tiers

---

### Freemium

**Definition:** Free tier with paid upgrades.

**Examples:**
- Spotify: Free with ads → Premium $10/month
- Dropbox: 2GB free → Paid plans for more storage

**Advantages:**
- Zero friction acquisition
- Viral growth potential
- Try before you buy
- Large user base for network effects

**Challenges:**
- Free users cost money to serve
- Low free-to-paid conversion (typically 2-5%)
- Cannibalization risk
- Must balance free vs paid value

**When to use:**
- Product-led growth strategy
- Low marginal cost to serve users
- Network effects or viral loops
- Value becomes clear through usage

**Conversion tactics:**
- Usage limits (hit the limit → upgrade)
- Feature gating (power features behind paywall)
- Collaboration (free individual, paid team features)
- Time limits (free trial → paid)

---

### Per-Seat Pricing

**Definition:** Charge per user.

**Examples:**
- Slack: $8/user/month
- Salesforce: $150/user/month

**Advantages:**
- Simple to understand
- Revenue grows with team growth
- Easy to calculate total cost

**Challenges:**
- Discourages adoption (team wants to add users)
- Seat sharing workarounds
- Doesn't align with value if non-users benefit
- Can create friction in sales process

**When to use:**
- Tool for individual productivity
- Each user extracts similar value
- Want linear revenue scaling

**Variations:**
- Active users only (fair for occasional use)
- Role-based pricing (power users pay more)
- Minimum seats with volume discounts

---

### Outcome-Based Pricing

**Definition:** Pay based on results achieved.

**Examples:**
- Performance marketing: Pay per lead/sale
- Recruiting: Pay when hire is made
- Legal: Contingency fees (% of settlement)

**Advantages:**
- Perfect value alignment
- Low customer risk
- Competitive differentiation
- Justifies premium pricing

**Challenges:**
- Hard to measure outcomes
- Revenue unpredictability
- Success depends on customer execution too
- May incentivize wrong behaviors

**When to use:**
- Outcomes are measurable
- You control most success variables
- High customer skepticism
- Want to differentiate from competitors

## Pricing Psychology

### Anchoring

**Principle:** First number sets reference point for all subsequent numbers.

**Applications:**

**Show higher price first:**
```
Annual: $1,000/year ($83/month) ← Shown first
Monthly: $99/month
```
Annual looks like a deal compared to monthly anchor.

**Decoy pricing:**
```
Basic: $29/month
Professional: $99/month ← Most want this
Enterprise: $399/month ← Makes Pro look reasonable
```

**Reference point:**
"Most companies spend $50,000/year on [problem]. Our solution costs $15,000."
$15k seems reasonable compared to $50k anchor.

---

### Charm Pricing

**Principle:** Prices ending in 9 or 99 seem significantly less than round numbers.

**Examples:**
- $99 feels much cheaper than $100
- $4,999 seems like "the $4,000s" not "almost $5,000"

**When to use:**
- Consumer products
- Price-sensitive markets
- Lower-priced offerings

**When NOT to use:**
- Luxury/premium positioning
- Enterprise B2B
- When projecting professionalism

---

### Price Framing

**Principle:** How you present price affects perception.

**Techniques:**

**Break down to smallest unit:**
```
$1,200/year → "Just $3.29 per day"
$50/month per user → "$2.50 per user per week"
```

**Show value ratio:**
```
"This solution saves $100,000/year and costs $30,000"
ROI: 3.3x
```

**Compare to alternatives:**
```
"Costs less than hiring one additional employee"
"Fraction of the cost of building internally"
```

**Emphasize gains vs losses:**
- Loss framing: "Don't lose $X in wasted time"
- Gain framing: "Save $X annually"
(Loss framing often more motivating)

---

### Payment Terms

**Annual prepayment discount:**
```
Monthly: $100/month ($1,200/year)
Annual: $1,000/year (2 months free)
```

**Why it works:**
- Reduces churn (commitment device)
- Improves cash flow
- Lower processing fees
- Discounting feels like getting a deal

**Typical discount:** 15-20% for annual prepayment

## Pricing Optimization

### Price Testing

**What to test:**
- Absolute price levels
- Packaging (which features in which tier?)
- Tier names and positioning
- Payment terms
- Discount structures

**A/B Testing Approach:**
```
Segment: New customers by geography
Variant A: $99/month
Variant B: $129/month

Measure:
- Conversion rate
- Total revenue
- Customer LTV
- Churn rates

Duration: 60-90 days (statistical significance)
```

**Caution:** Existing customers seeing different prices can create negative sentiment.

---

### Pricing Page Optimization

**Elements to test:**

**Value proposition clarity:**
- Lead with outcomes, not features
- Quantify value where possible
- Show before/after transformation

**Pricing display:**
- Annual vs monthly default
- Tier ordering
- Highlight most popular tier

**Social proof:**
- Customer logos
- Reviews/testimonials
- Usage stats ("10,000+ companies")

**CTAs:**
- "Start free trial" vs "Get started" vs "Request demo"
- Button color and placement

**FAQ addressing objections:**
- "Can I cancel anytime?"
- "Do I need a credit card for trial?"
- "What if I outgrow my plan?"

---

### Segment-Specific Pricing

**Different prices for different customer types.**

**Segmentation Dimensions:**

**Company size:**
- SMB: $500/month
- Mid-market: $2,500/month
- Enterprise: $10,000+/month

**Geography:**
- US: Full price
- Europe: Adjusted for purchasing power
- Emerging markets: Discounted

**Use case:**
- Commercial use: Full price
- Education: 50% discount
- Non-profit: 75% discount

**Customer type:**
- New customers: Promotional pricing
- Existing customers: Grandfathered rates
- Enterprise: Custom pricing

**Implementation:**
- Show different pricing pages by segment
- Qualify before showing price
- Use discount codes
- Custom quotes

## Common Pricing Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Cost-Plus Pricing for Differentiated Products

**Problem:** Pricing based on costs ignores customer value.

**Example:**
```
Cost to deliver: $20
Margin: 40%
Price: $33

But customer would pay $100 for the value delivered.
Leaving $67 on the table!
```

**Fix:** Use value-based pricing. Understand what customers gain, price accordingly.

---

### Mistake 2: Race to the Bottom

**Problem:** Competing primarily on price.

**Result:**
- Unsustainable margins
- Attracts worst customers (price-sensitive, high churn)
- Can't invest in product
- Positions as "cheap"

**Fix:** Compete on value, not price. Differentiate, then price for that differentiation.

---

### Mistake 3: Underpricing

**Problem:** Setting price too low out of fear customers won't pay.

**Signals:**
- 100% of prospects say "yes" to price
- No price objections
- Customers seem surprised by low price

**Cost:**
- Money left on the table
- Longer path to profitability
- Can't fund growth

**Fix:** Test higher prices. You can always lower prices; raising them is harder.

---

### Mistake 4: Too Many Pricing Tiers

**Problem:** 6+ tiers confuse customers.

**Result:**
- Analysis paralysis
- Customers don't know which to choose
- Low conversion

**Fix:** 3-4 tiers maximum. Clear differentiation.

---

### Mistake 5: Anchoring on the Wrong Number

**Problem:** First number presented is your lowest price.

**Example:**
```
Starter: $29 ← First thing they see
Professional: $99
Enterprise: $299
```

Customer anchors on $29, $99 seems expensive.

**Fix:** Show annually first, or lead with value statement before showing price tiers.

---

### Mistake 6: Feature Parity with Free

**Problem:** Free tier has almost everything, little reason to upgrade.

**Fix:** Gate high-value features:
- Collaboration features (free = individual, paid = team)
- Advanced features (free = basic, paid = power features)
- Usage limits (free = 100/month, paid = unlimited)

---

### Mistake 7: No Price Increases

**Problem:** Never raising prices erodes margins over time.

**Reality:**
- Costs increase (salaries, infrastructure)
- Product improves (more value)
- Market pays more (competitors raise prices)

**Fix:** Annual price increases for new customers (3-5%). Grandfather existing customers or give advance notice.

## Pricing Changes

### When to Change Pricing

**Signals you're underpriced:**
- Converting 80%+ of trials
- No price objections
- Customers surprised by low price
- LTV:CAC ratio > 5:1
- Leaving money on the table

**Signals you're overpriced:**
- High drop-off at pricing page
- Constant price objections
- Lost deals primarily to price
- LTV:CAC ratio < 2:1

**External triggers:**
- Competitors changed pricing
- Market conditions shifted
- New features justify more value
- Costs increased significantly

---

### How to Raise Prices

**For new customers:**
- Easiest approach
- No backlash
- Announce publicly: "Starting [date], new pricing is..."

**For existing customers:**

**Option 1: Grandfather Indefinitely**
- Existing customers keep old pricing forever
- New customers pay new pricing
- **Pros:** No backlash, loyalty reward
- **Cons:** Two-tier system complexity

**Option 2: Price Increase with Notice**
- 60-90 days advance notice
- Existing customers move to new pricing
- **Pros:** Simplicity, fairness
- **Cons:** Some churn risk

**Option 3: Optional Upgrade**
- New features only available at new price
- Existing customers can stay on old plan
- **Pros:** Customers choose, less resistance
- **Cons:** Fragmentation, complexity

**Communication:**
```
Subject: Upcoming Pricing Changes

We're excited to announce significant product improvements:
[List new features/value]

To support continued innovation, we're updating our pricing starting [date]:
[New pricing]

Current customers can:
- Keep current pricing until [date]
- Lock in current rate by switching to annual
- Contact us with any concerns

We're grateful for your business and committed to delivering exceptional value.
```

## Industry-Specific Pricing Considerations

### B2B SaaS

**Typical Model:** Tiered subscription + usage/seat-based
**ACV Range:** $1k-$100k+
**Key Metrics:** MRR/ARR, CAC payback, Net Revenue Retention

**Pricing Levers:**
- Per seat/user
- Usage tiers
- Feature differentiation
- Service levels (support, SLAs)

---

### E-commerce

**Typical Model:** Cost-plus with competitive adjustment
**Margin Range:** 30-70% depending on category
**Key Metrics:** AOV, repeat purchase rate, LTV

**Pricing Levers:**
- Dynamic pricing (algorithmic)
- Bundling
- Volume discounts
- Seasonal promotions

---

### Marketplace

**Typical Model:** Take rate (% of transaction value)
**Take Rate:** 10-30% typical
**Key Metrics:** GMV, take rate, buyer/seller retention

**Pricing Levers:**
- Transaction fees
- Subscription for sellers
- Featured listings
- Value-added services

---

### Consulting/Services

**Typical Model:** Hourly/project/value-based
**Rate Range:** $100-$500+/hour depending on expertise
**Key Metrics:** Utilization rate, effective rate, client LTV

**Pricing Levers:**
- Hourly rates by seniority
- Project-based (fixed scope)
- Retainers (predictable revenue)
- Value-based (outcomes)
